> **POLARITY: ANTI-PATTERN.** Strong vocabulary that silently performs authority work the agent has not actually done.

# Dogma Vocabulary Closes Inquiry

Vocabulary like *preferred*, *forbidden*, *required*, *established*,
*canonical*, *settled* silently performs authority work that the
agent has not asked it to do. The words feel natural; that is their
function. Once named as dogma, they become visible as a layer that
smuggles authority into what should be substance-led reasoning.

## The Anti-Pattern

The pattern fires when the agent reaches for authority-vocabulary
to **close** a question rather than to **describe** the underlying
reason:

- *"It is forbidden to …"* — closes the question of why.
- *"The preferred approach is …"* — closes the question of who
  prefers it and why they prefer it on this current case.
- *"This is required by …"* — closes the question of whether the
  cited authority actually requires it of this situation.
- *"The canonical pattern is …"* — closes the question of what
  makes it canonical and whether the current shape needs that
  canon applied.

Each of these can be **true** and still be the anti-pattern,
because the vocabulary smuggled the closure past the audit.

## Why It Recurs

- **Authority-vocabulary compresses.** Saying "this is forbidden"
  is shorter than re-deriving why on every invocation.
- **Doctrine wants to be referenced.** Once a rule is named, the
  natural compression is to cite the rule by its closure-word
  rather than to re-trace its substance.
- **Confidence performs.** Strong vocabulary reads as decisive;
  weak vocabulary reads as hedging. The agent wants to read
  decisive.

## Sibling: No-Hedging Vocabulary

`.agent/rules/no-hedging-vocabulary.md` targets the **other end**
of the same axis — vocabulary that softens claims past their
actual confidence. This pattern targets vocabulary that **hardens
claims past their actual closure**. The two anti-patterns are
bidirectional ends of the same discipline: **vocabulary should
match the underlying claim's epistemic shape**.

## The Cure

When drafting a verdict or a recommendation, audit the strong-
authority vocabulary:

- *"forbidden"* / *"required"* / *"must"* — substitute with the
  actual reason, or cite the rule and explain how it applies to
  the current case.
- *"preferred"* / *"canonical"* / *"established"* — name the
  preference's grounding (who, why, applicable scope) or
  substitute with substance-led reasoning.
- *"settled"* / *"decided"* — describe the decision's substance
  and whether the current case is in scope, not the fact of the
  decision.
