# PDR-084: Owner Action Is Not a Cure

## Context

Owner intervention often appears at the moment a blocked team starts
moving again: the owner grants permission, re-routes agents, names the
right frame, approves a principle, or cuts through coordination
deadlock. The visible event is owner action. The durable learning is
rarely "ask the owner next time".

If the Practice records owner action as the cure, it trains agents to
externalise autonomy. Future agents learn to wait for the owner when
they should instead improve the substrate that would have made the
move safe without owner intervention.

That failure is subtle because some decisions genuinely are owner-only.
The cure is not to bypass the owner. The cure is to classify the
intervention correctly.

## Decision

Owner action is evidence to classify, not a cure to copy.

Every owner intervention that unblocks work must be classified into
one of two shapes:

1. **Owner-only decision**: the decision depends on preference,
   priority, risk appetite, business context, or authority that only
   the owner can supply. The durable cure is to preserve the decision
   and route any reusable principle to the correct home.
2. **Missing autonomy primitive**: the team could have resolved the
   situation safely if the Practice substrate had supplied a protocol,
   rule, affordance, evidence path, role boundary, or default. The
   durable cure is to name and route that missing primitive.

"Ask the owner next time" is valid only for owner-only decisions. It
is invalid as the cure for missing autonomy primitives.

## Diagnostic Test

After an owner action unblocks work, ask:

1. What decision did the owner supply?
2. Could the team have made that decision safely with a clearer
   protocol, better evidence, a standing rule, or a known role
   boundary?
3. If yes, what substrate was missing?
4. If no, what owner-only authority made the decision owner-bound?
5. Where does the reusable learning belong: PDR, ADR, rule, skill,
   pattern, directive, open question, or pending graduation?

The classification must be recorded at the smallest useful surface.
If no clear home exists, the result is an explicit home-gap discussion,
not quiet compression into a session note.

## Rationale

The owner is a source of direction and authority. The owner is not the
Practice's substitute for missing protocol.

Rejected alternatives:

- **Always ask the owner**: safe in the narrow moment, but it makes
  autonomy worse over time and increases owner-attention cost.
- **Never ask the owner**: incorrectly treats preference, priority,
  authority, and risk appetite as inferable substrate when they are
  not.
- **Record the incident only as history**: preserves what happened
  but fails to extract the reusable distinction between owner-only
  authority and missing autonomy primitive.

## Consequences

### Required

- Owner interventions that unblock work are reviewed for substrate
  implications during consolidation, handoff, or Director
  substrate-writing.
- Agents distinguish owner-only decisions from missing autonomy
  primitives before writing a durable cure.
- Missing autonomy primitives route to their correct home:
  protocol, rule, skill, PDR, ADR, tool affordance, or pending
  graduation.
- Owner-only decisions are preserved with their authority intact and
  not rewritten as agent-derived doctrine.

### Forbidden

- Treating owner intervention itself as the durable cure when the
  underlying problem was missing substrate.
- Adding "ask the owner" defaults to protocols where a team-owned
  evidence path or role boundary would make the move safe.
- Bypassing the owner for genuinely owner-only decisions by claiming
  autonomy.
- Compressing unclear interventions into vague lessons instead of
  surfacing the classification question.

### Accepted Costs

- Consolidation takes slightly longer because owner interventions
  require classification.
- Some cases remain unresolved until the owner clarifies whether the
  decision was owner-only or substrate-addressable.
- The Practice may create more small protocol or rule follow-ons.
  That is acceptable when those follow-ons reduce future owner
  attention without erasing owner authority.

## Falsifiability

This PDR is falsified if classifying owner interventions into these
two shapes does not reduce repeated owner interventions of the same
kind, or if it causes agents to overreach into owner-only decisions.
It is also falsified if the classification cannot be applied by a
future agent without reconstructing private context.
