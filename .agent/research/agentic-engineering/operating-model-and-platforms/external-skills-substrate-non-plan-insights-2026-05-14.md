## Purpose

This note records insights from the external substrate study that should not be
forced into executable planning language. They may inform future doctrine,
review, or research, but they are not themselves tasks.

## Metacognitive Insight

The important distinction is between **substrate influence** and **work item**.
A concept can change how we see the Practice without becoming a planned slice.
Forcing every observation into a plan creates false commitments and makes
research look more certain than it is.

## Insights That Do Not Naturally Sit In A Plan

### 1. Portability Makes Prompts Thin

The external repo's portability is partly a strength and partly a constraint.
Because it has to travel lightly, much of its substrate lives in memorable
phrases and skill instructions. Oak should learn from the compression, not from
the thinness. Our stronger substrate should remain stateful where shared work
needs state.

### 2. Small Names Can Carry Large Behaviour

Names like "diagnose", "prototype", "handoff", and "grill" are not just labels.
They are mode switches. The Practice has many richer concepts, but some are
harder to invoke in ordinary conversation. This is not immediately a plan item;
it is a design pressure on future skill and rule authors.

### 3. A Glossary Is Different From Memory

A glossary-like surface is useful when the problem is repeated term negotiation,
not when the problem is remembering decisions. Oak should avoid treating every
local vocabulary issue as memory debt. Sometimes the right artefact is a small
domain language cache.

### 4. Negative Memory Needs Revisit Conditions

Rejected or deferred ideas are useful only if they record why they were rejected
and when the decision should be revisited. Without revisit conditions, negative
memory becomes a quiet ban list. This is a principle to carry into any future
negative-decision memory design.

### 5. Handoff Compression Is A Knowledge-Economy Signal

The best handoff is not the longest one. It is the one that points to the right
authoritative surfaces and leaves only the missing operational delta. This
insight should inform closeout prose even before any handoff workflow changes.

### 6. Prompt-Only Guardrails Are A Useful Warning

The external repo shows how much can be achieved with clear prompt-level
instructions, but also where prompt-only governance stops. In Oak, the more a
behaviour affects shared state, ownership, or truth claims, the more it should
move from prose into tools, checks, state, or workflow gates.

### 7. Source Study Findings Should Stay Source-Neutral

The tracked research value is the substrate pattern, not the identity of the
source. Future references should prefer the neutral report and the ignored local
snapshot only when exact wording is required for private analysis.

### 8. Candidate Count Is Not Progress

The study produced many interesting concepts. The Practice benefits only when a
candidate has a failure mode, a natural home, and a proof signal. A smaller
register with firm verdicts is better than a broad backlog of attractive names.

## Deferred Observation

The study suggests a possible future "external substrate review" template:

- identify explicit substrate surfaces;
- infer implicit flows;
- compare against Practice layers;
- separate learnings from adoption candidates;
- record non-plan insights separately.

That template should wait until at least one more external-substrate study
repeats the shape.
