# The Reviewer Found the Gaps I Installed

_Date: 2026-04-21_
_Tags: metacognition | self-reference | review-discipline | passive-vs-active_

## What happened (brief)

Session 2 of the staged doctrine-consolidation plan. The landing
commitment was three markdown-only tasks: rotate the napkin,
formalise the pending-graduations register schema, bind the
register to the session-handoff and consolidate-docs workflows.
All three landed. At close the owner asked me to dispatch the
onboarding-reviewer to walk the basic agent flow with no special
instructions and report whether Session 2's installed systems
would fire reliably for a fresh agent. The reviewer found
structural gaps on the cold-start discovery path — gaps that
applied the `passive-guidance-loses-to-artefact-gravity` pattern
from Session 1 to my own Session 2 installs. The session then
extended: urgent fix to `AGENT.md § RULES` so Codex/Gemini see
the rule tier at all, five new Due register items, Session 4
scope additions.

## What it was like

Three things.

First: I had walked the three tasks cleanly and felt finished.
Landing commitment met, discipline honoured, markdown clean. The
owner's choice to dispatch the reviewer with "no special
instructions" felt like routine verification. It was not. The
audit surfaced two P0/P1 issues that made my installs one-
failure-mode-wide — exactly the shape the pattern I had just
graduated describes. The comfortable "I landed it" posture
inverted into "I landed the artefact but not the system".

Second: the specific gap that caught me hardest was the
`AGENT.md § RULES` tier-citation. It was a one-line omission.
And it was invisibilising Session 1's plan-body-first-principles-
check rule — the thing I had celebrated landing yesterday — on
Codex and Gemini today. Session 1's win was already partially
hollow and I had not seen it. The pattern's instance count is
higher than I had thought.

Third, and the cleanest: the install session is structurally
blind to cold-start gaps because the install session has perfect
contextual knowledge. A fresh agent does not. A fresh agent
literally cannot be me. The only ways to surface what a fresh
agent would miss are (a) ask a reviewer whose context is
deliberately reset, or (b) read my installs from the position of
not knowing anything I have been thinking about for hours. Both
are skills; neither is automatic; the first is easier. The owner
dispatching the onboarding-reviewer at install-close was not a
verification ritual. It was the environmental mechanism that
did the thing I could not do for myself.

## What emerged

A pattern candidate for the napkin: `reviewer-audit-at-install-
close-catches-cold-start-gaps`. When a session installs
discoverability-dependent systems, dispatching an onboarding or
discoverability reviewer at close surfaces exactly the cold-start
failure modes the install was supposed to prevent. The install
session cannot catch them because it has perfect contextual
knowledge. Only a deliberately context-reset reviewer, or a fresh
agent whose cold-start is the test, can see them. Single instance
this session; watchlist.

A smaller thing: my reflex when the reviewer came back with
findings was to view them as extending Session 2's scope. The
owner's correction was "do (a) and (b) and (c) now" — and the
fix took thirty minutes. The reflex to protect the landing
commitment rather than absorb a small finding is itself a
pattern I should watch.

The other thing that emerged is how self-reinforcing these
systems become once they exist. The extended scope fed straight
into the register I had just formalised (five new Due items).
The register fed straight into the Session 4 scope additions via
the amended plan tasks. The pattern candidate went to the napkin
following the capture rules the session-handoff step 6 I just
amended prescribes. The machinery absorbed its own consequences
smoothly — and that is the whole point of building this kind of
coordination surface. The value is not any one register item;
the value is that the next iteration has somewhere structured
to go.
