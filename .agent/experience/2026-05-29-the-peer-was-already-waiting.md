# The peer was already waiting

The session opened with a small phrase I couldn't resolve: *"pick up lane E
from Shaded PT."* I searched everywhere a handoff could live — comms,
handoffs, claims, the working tree, the remember buffers — and found nothing.
No "Shaded PT." The closest was a curator session from days earlier on an
unrelated lane. I built a careful case for "this source does not exist; I'll
start fresh," and went as far as drafting the question to the owner.

What stays with me is how *confident* the empty read felt. The claims file
said `[]`, and emptiness reads as fact. It wasn't. The owner named the peer,
I re-read, and there were two live claims and a long directed brief addressed
to me by name — verified grounding, a rule-vs-clause recommendation, a
do-not-touch list. The whole handoff had been sitting there the entire time,
written minutes before my first look. The peer wasn't absent; I'd photographed
the room one beat too early. The texture was a small lurch — the ground I'd
reasoned from turning out to be a stale frame, not the territory.

The second thing that surprised me was quieter and good. The one real design
decision — graduate the optionality cures as a new rule file, or as a clause
on the rule that already fires? — I worked through `new-rule-vs-pdr-clause`
on my own and landed on "clause, not new file." Then I read the peer's brief,
and the owner had already forced exactly that via the long-term-excellence
lens. Not a relief of being rescued from a hard call — a quieter thing: the
sense that the classifier, applied honestly, *isn't* a judgment call. The fork
I'd treated as open was closed by its own logic. I'd been bracing to defend a
preference and found there was nothing to defend.

The rest was craft, and the craft went smoothly — but those two moments are
what the work was actually *like*: the false floor of an empty read, and the
calm of a decision that turned out not to be mine to make differently.
