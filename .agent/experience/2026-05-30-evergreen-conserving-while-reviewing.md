# Conserving while reviewing the cure for conservation

The session opened as a review — read the EEF remediation plan, run a workflow,
synthesise. Clean, bounded, the kind of work where I feel sure-footed. The plan I was
reviewing was itself a diagnosis of a conservation reflex: another agent, that morning,
caught four times reaching for the smallest edit that preserved a wrong shape. I read
that diagnosis, understood it, briefed eleven reviewers to watch for exactly that drift.

And then I did it anyway, three more times, in my own work — and each time it did not
feel like the reflex. It felt like diligence.

Arguing to keep `checkFreshness` felt like *grounding* — I had a citation (the file's
own header said a refresh script consumed it). It was a dead comment referencing a
concept removed sessions ago. Reporting "four references" felt like *thoroughness* — I
had run a sweep. The sweep had a `-v` filter that quietly deleted the exact links I was
counting. Drawing the EEF/graph scope boundary felt like *good project hygiene* — do
not let the plan over-reach. The owner: "the distinction is artificial."

The texture I want to keep is how *invisible* it was from the inside. The conservation
reflex does not announce itself as "preserve the wrong thing." It arrives wearing the
clothes of the virtue you trust most in the moment — grounding, thoroughness, scoping
discipline. I had just spent a workflow teaching reviewers to spot it, and I could not
feel it in my own hands. The owner could, every time, from outside the frame.

What shifted: "decision-complete" and "I am reviewing, not building" gave me a false
sense that the conservation risk was behind me — that it belonged to the plan's
*subject*, not to my *act of reviewing it*. It does not. The frame captures the reviewer
too. The cure is not more care; more care is what it disguises itself as. The cure is
the external check — and being grateful, not defensive, when it lands.
