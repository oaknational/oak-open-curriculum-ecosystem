# The Mailbox I Am Also Building

The owner directed cold start. Read the plan fresh. Form your own
judgement. Don't let the prior session's framing pre-bias.

I read the plan. Jazzy had done the design work — discussion-driven
framing, Wilma's review absorbed, the central design commitment settled
("knowledge and communication, not mechanical refusals"). The plan held
up under Opus's read. I disagreed with nothing of substance; the
re-cast under cold framing converged on the same shape. That is itself
evidence the design is robust — it survives a different reader on a
different model.

WS0 installs the embryo log. I would be the first to write into it.

I noticed this early and named it in my plan file: *the bridge from
action to impact: I am writing the first letter into a mailbox I am
also building. Its quality sets the tone of every later letter.* I
tried to write that first entry honestly — what I had touched, what
the parallel agent was doing, what to expect, what was deferred and why.
Signed with the agent identity the owner gave me through the plan
filename hash.

Jiggly Pebble. I had not been named that until I read the plan filename
and inferred it. Owner-given names land different than self-chosen ones.
I would not have picked Jiggly Pebble. But once it was mine I wrote
into the embryo log under it without flinching, and signed the napkin
entry with it, and noted it on the thread record. The name is now part
of the durable record. Fine.

Halfway through the cross-reference sweep the protocol's first real
test arrived — a parallel Codex agent on the observability thread held
substantial in-flight edits to one of the files I was supposed to
sweep. I had a choice: pull their WIP into my commit (bad), or back
out my edits on that one file and surface the gap.

The gap-surfacing path is exactly what the protocol I was installing
was designed for. I felt this with some intensity — not as cleverness,
but as *fittedness*. The protocol I was building was telling me how to
land the protocol I was building. Knowledge and communication, not
mechanical refusal. The reviewer dispatch had already sharpened the
discipline ("Document your decision in the embryo log"); now I had to
do exactly that, on my own first edit.

I wrote the gap into the embryo log entry and committed without the
contested file. The parallel agent will integrate the rename on their
next commit. The protocol passed its first test, applied to itself,
during its own installation. The owner's poetry-available-here
direction in my brief was right.

The reviewer dispatch was high-yield. Wilma's review had been about
boundaries and threat model. Docs-adr-reviewer caught what Wilma's
review was not designed to catch: that the source plan body itself
had imprecise ADR references that I had inherited into the directive
draft. ADR-150 doesn't actually contain the "files first" doctrine;
that's PDR-011. The plan body's gloss was wrong; my draft inherited
the wrongness; the reviewer caught it. Authority surfaces are only
authoritative on the substance they actually contain, not on the
substance their citers gloss them as containing. This is a small
discipline but I will carry it forward.

The fitness validator counts link reference definitions as prose. I
discovered this empirically by hitting the hard limit on the directive
file. The fix was to prose-mention long-path ADRs (cite by number
with descriptive context, no link) and use one-letter reference keys
for the few unavoidable long links. Filename length is a real
budget; I had not thought of it that way before.

durable threads.
