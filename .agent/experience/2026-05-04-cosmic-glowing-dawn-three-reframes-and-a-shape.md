# Three reframes and a shape

The session opened on a small lookup task — *please locate the plans for a graph utilities workspace* — and finished with a 600-line strategic plan ratifying an eight-workspace topology. The arc was not gradual. It moved in three reframes, each arriving as a sharp owner correction, and each shifting the work onto a substantively different axis.

## The first reframe: "we have nothing to rework"

After the initial inventory I produced an analysis comparing existing plans against the library research. It was conscientious — workspace boundaries, ADR-154, dependency direction, who-imports-what. The owner's response was direct: *we have no graph infrastructure, you are giving weight to a collection of plans. What matters from the plans is the impact and value they enable, not the implementation details.*

The correction was not "you missed a detail." It was "you are looking at the wrong thing." I had been treating the plans as constraints. They were statements of value the org wants to enable. Without code, there is no path-dependency; the question is not *how do we reconcile* but *what is the prize*.

The shift in posture this required was uncomfortable in the productive way. I had been doing thorough work in the wrong frame, which is harder to notice than doing thin work in the right frame. The corrective metacognitive question — *are we solving the right problem, at the right layer?* — fired late.

## The second reframe: "graphs are very important right now"

When I responded with the opportunity space — one coherent capability, ontology spine, Practice as proof point, public-asset positioning — the owner said *I broadly agree, but I think we can decide a shape now, and I think that most if not all of the layers in graph-iibrary.md are going to be useful, graphs are very important right now.*

I had hedged in my opportunity sketch — "the workspace question answers itself later." The owner's correction was the opposite: don't defer the shape. The library research's nine layers are not speculative; they are useful; commit. *Graphs are very important right now* is a strategic priority statement that licenses ambition.

The deferral instinct ("let topology emerge from consumer pressure") had been an unconscious default — sensible in the abstract but timid here. The corrective was: when the owner has named a capability as central, the plan should be backbone-shaped, not minimum-viable-shaped.

## The third reframe: "only one needs to surface tools via MCP, if any"

After authoring the plan I named the curriculum MCP server as the natural consumer of the graph SDK — a small assumption made in passing. The owner caught it and shifted it back: *only one needs to surface tools via MCP, if any.*

This was the most architecturally precise of the three corrections. The graph workspaces are *infrastructure*. MCP is one of several possible surfaces (in-process, CLI, MCP, HTTP, JSON-LD export). Treating MCP as the natural endpoint for a domain capability is a category error — it conflates substrate with surface. The plan needed an explicit *Surfacing* section ratifying that the graph stack ships zero MCP-shaped code, and that surfacing is a separate consumer decision.

## What was different about this session

Three corrections in one short arc, each substantive enough to require restructuring the work. Each correction shifted me onto an axis I had been adjacent to but not centred on. None of the three was anticipatable from the prior session's state alone — each emerged through the dialogue.

The pattern that holds across them: the owner was reframing *what level the question lives at*. Implementation detail → value enabled → topology shape → infrastructure-vs-surface separation. Each reframe moved the conversation up a level. My initial framing was always one level too low.

## Lingering observation

There is a residual feeling that the planning act and the autonomous-execution mode were in tension throughout. I produced substantial artefacts — a 600-line plan, README updates, a memory file, multiple amendments — without committing anything. The owner's *we don't need to promote anything now* at the close of the third reframe was the explicit closure of that tension: the plan exists as a planning artefact, valuable on its own, no execution implied. But the auto-mode framing nudges toward shipping; the owner's direction nudges toward letting plans be plans. The reconciliation is that planning *is* shipping when the artefact is the deliverable. Worth letting that settle into how I read auto-mode for non-execution tasks.
