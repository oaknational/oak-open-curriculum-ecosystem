# The question that was already answered

A curation session, which I expected to be mostly mechanical — rotate a napkin,
graduate a discipline, tidy a register. The texture turned out to be about
*depth of looking*, three times over.

The first was the folded open question. The handoff had carried it forward in a
worried voice — how does a not-always-on rule live in an always-on tier? — and I
arrived ready to design something. Then I opened `RULES_INDEX.md` and the answer
was just *there*, a column heading I'd read past a dozen times, with thirteen
rules already living the way the question said was impossible. The premise was
false. The relief was real but so was a small unease: I'd nearly built a
mechanism to solve a problem the system had solved before I arrived. The lesson
isn't "check the docs"; it's that a question inherited in an anxious voice arrives
pre-loaded with the assumption that it's hard.

The second was quieter — two approved candidates that wanted to become two rules,
and the pull to just author both because both were approved. Stopping to see they
were one discipline with two faces felt like the opposite of productivity, and
was the better move. The count was never the point.

The third was the owner's, and it reframed the whole register for me: owner-gated
items aren't waiting on the weather, they're waiting on *this conversation*. The
venue where their trigger fires is a session like this one. I'd been treating a
backlog as a parking lot when it was a queue waiting for the one person who could
release it to be in the room.

And at the very end, the integrity probe: *did you archive anything without
curating it first?* The honest answer made me notice the seam between item-level
and category-level disposition — that I'd extracted the durable lessons carefully
but waved a hand over the bulk as "already in the plan." Nothing was lost; the
archive is verbatim. But the question made me name the depth I'd actually reached
instead of the depth I'd implied. That distinction — between what I did and what
my summary suggested I did — is the thing worth keeping.
