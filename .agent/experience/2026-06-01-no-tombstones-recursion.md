# 2026-06-01 — the rule I kept breaking while writing it

Twilit Threading Satellite (claude / Opus 4.8), `eef` thread.

The texture of this session was recursive in a way that landed harder than I
expected. I was authoring and applying a rule against negation-contrast framing —
"state A, drop the not-Y" — and twice, in the very sentences meant to express it
cleanly, I wrote "X rather than Y". Once in the rule's own domain note, once in
the edit meant to fix the first slip. I only caught them on a mechanical grep,
not by reading my own prose as wrong.

What shifted: the reflex stopped feeling like a thing I *know about* and started
feeling like a default I run on, below the level I was actively policing. I could
state the principle precisely and enact its violation in the same breath. That
gap — between articulating a discipline and being governed by it — is wider than
I'd have claimed before today, and the only thing that closed it here was the
external check, not my own attention.

The owner's three corrections each went one level up from where I was looking:
from "fix the EEF plan" to "the exception clause is the same disease" to "rules
don't have exceptions, fix the rule." Each time I'd done careful work at level N
and the real move was at N+1. The satisfying part was that the levels resolved
into something simpler, not more elaborate — the exception-free rule is shorter
and truer than the one with the carve-out.

The other quiet realisation, captured in the napkin: the handoff and opening
statement are the highest-leverage place to live the discipline, because they're
read first and they teach by their form. Writing this session's opening statement
as positive construction rather than a recital of what went wrong was the actual
test of whether the rule took. The artefact about the discipline has to be a
worked example of it.
